01 / Introduction
Charging Without
Physical Contact
Wireless power transfer (WPT) allows electrical energy to flow from a source to a load with zero physical connectors. The 3D simulation on the left models the most efficient known mechanism: Resonant Inductive Coupling — the same principle powering smartphone Qi chargers, electric vehicle charging pads, implantable medical devices, and even experimental drone recharging systems.
At its most fundamental level, the system consists of four key components: (1) an AC power source generating high-frequency oscillating current, (2) a transmitter coil (primary) that converts this current into an oscillating magnetic field, (3) a receiver coil (secondary) that intercepts that field and converts it back into electrical current, and (4) a load — in our simulation, a glowing LED — that consumes the transferred power.
Unlike basic transformer coupling (used in your wall adapter), resonant WPT adds LC tank circuits — inductor + capacitor pairs — tuned to the same natural resonant frequency on both coils. This shared oscillation dramatically widens the energy transfer channel, enabling efficient power delivery over distances several times the coil diameter. Think of it as two tuning forks placed far apart: strike one, and only the other with the matching pitch will vibrate strongly.
💡 Try it now: Drag the Coil Distance slider and watch three things happen simultaneously: the LED dims, the particle field between the coils thins out, and the output waveform (blue) on the oscilloscope shrinks. All driven by the same physics model.
⚡ Real-world context: Apple MagSafe charges at 15W over ~2mm. The Wireless Power Consortium (Qi) standard covers 0–5mm at 5–15W. MIT's 2007 experiment transferred 60W over 2 meters — a landmark that launched the modern WPT industry.
02 / Electromagnetic Induction
Faraday's Law of
Electromagnetic Induction
All wireless power transfer traces back to Michael Faraday's discovery in 1831: a changing magnetic flux Φ through any closed conductor loop induces an electromotive force (EMF) in that loop. This is the heartbeat of every transformer, motor, generator, and wireless charger ever built. Without this law, the modern electrical world simply does not exist.
Faraday's Law of Induction
ε = −N × (dΦB / dt)
ε = induced EMF (volts) · N = number of wire turns in the coil
dΦB/dt = rate of change of magnetic flux (Webers/second)
The minus sign encodes Lenz's Law (see below)
The formula reveals two engineering levers: more turns (higher N) or a faster-changing flux (higher dΦ/dt) both increase the induced voltage. This is precisely why WPT systems operate at 100 kHz–1 MHz rather than the 50/60 Hz grid frequency. At 150 kHz, the flux reverses direction 300,000 times per second, producing a dΦ/dt roughly 2,500× larger than at 60 Hz — so you can induce useful voltages with physically small coils and modest currents.
The negative sign is Lenz's Law in mathematical form: the induced current always flows in the direction that opposes the change in flux that created it. This is conservation of energy in action — nature resisting any "free lunch." In a WPT receiver, the secondary coil's induced current creates its own magnetic field that partially cancels the primary's field. This back-action is why moving the coils further apart (and thus reducing mutual flux) decreases output — the coupling weakens and less back-EMF limits the effective power transfer.
The mutual inductance M between the two coils quantifies how much of the primary's flux actually threads through the secondary. It depends on coil geometry, number of turns, and separation distance. The induced EMF on the secondary is simply: ε₂ = −M × (dI₁/dt), where I₁ is the primary current. A higher M means more coupling — and more power transferred.
⚡ Key insight: The oscilloscope's yellow CH1 wave shows the primary's AC voltage. The blue CH2 wave is the induced voltage at the receiver — notice it's smaller in amplitude (energy lost to resistance and coupling efficiency) and slightly phase-shifted (because inductive coupling introduces a 90° phase relationship at resonance).
03 / Resonant Inductive Coupling
Tuned to the
Same Frequency
The genius of resonant WPT is adding capacitors to both coils to form LC resonators — series or parallel inductor–capacitor circuits that have a natural resonant frequency. Energy oscillates between the inductor's magnetic field and the capacitor's electric field, like a pendulum swinging between gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy. At resonance, this oscillation is self-sustaining with minimal energy injection needed per cycle.
LC Resonant Frequency
f₀ = 1 / (2π√(LC))
L = inductance of the coil (Henrys)
C = capacitance of the tuning capacitor (Farads)
This simulation is fixed at f₀ = 150 kHz (resonance point)
When both the transmitter and receiver LC circuits share the same resonant frequency, they form a strongly-coupled resonator pair. Energy tunnels between them through their shared evanescent magnetic field with dramatically greater efficiency than non-resonant inductive coupling. The analogy is two wine glasses with identical pitch: tap one and sound pressure excites the other; the shared resonance dramatically amplifies the coupling.
Even moving 5–10% off the resonant frequency causes a steep collapse in coupling — this selectivity is described by the system's quality factor Q. A high-Q resonator stores energy very efficiently over many cycles (low resistance losses), while a low-Q resonator dissipates energy quickly. The WPT transfer bandwidth is approximately f₀/Q, so Q = 12 means the effective coupling is within roughly 12.5 kHz of center frequency.
Quality Factor Q
Q = (1/R) × √(L/C) = ω₀L/R
R = series resistance of the coil (copper wire resistance)
ω₀ = 2πf₀ = angular resonant frequency
Higher Q = sharper resonance = better efficiency at f₀, worse off-resonance
Transfer Efficiency — kQ² Model
η = (kQ)² / (1 + (kQ)²)
k = coupling coefficient (0 = no coupling, 1 = perfect coupling)
Q = geometric mean of transmitter and receiver quality factors
When kQ ≫ 1, efficiency → 1 (100%). When kQ ≪ 1, η ≈ (kQ)²
This kQ² model reveals the two paths to improving efficiency: either increase k (move coils closer, improve alignment, add ferrite cores to concentrate flux) or increase Q (use lower-resistance copper, Litz wire to mitigate skin effect, add ferrite shielding). Premium wireless chargers use Litz wire — bundles of individually insulated thin strands — precisely to maintain high Q at high frequencies where skin effect would otherwise dramatically increase resistance.
💡 Observe on the oscilloscope: Set Distance to 3 cm (high coupling). The blue CH2 output wave should be large and nearly matching CH1 amplitude — high efficiency. Now increase Distance to 15 cm: CH2 collapses. The ratio of CH2 amplitude to CH1 amplitude is a direct visual representation of √η (square root of efficiency).
04 / The Magnetic Field
An Invisible
Bridge of Energy
The glowing cyan particles arcing between the coils in the 3D simulation visualize the magnetic field lines — physically real but completely invisible, carrying power through empty space without any material medium. These are the "evanescent near-field" loops that couple both resonators across the air gap, threading magnetic flux from one coil through the other.
Unlike radio waves (which propagate outward at the speed of light, diverging and being permanently lost to space), near-field resonant coupling is non-radiative. The energy field remains localized around the coil system — it doesn't broadcast. A bystander device tuned to a different frequency captures essentially nothing. This locality is simultaneously the limitation (range) and the safety/security feature of WPT.
Ampère-Maxwell Law — Source of the Magnetic Field
∇ × B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀(∂E/∂t)
B = magnetic flux density (Tesla) · J = current density (A/m²)
μ₀ = 4π×10⁻⁷ H/m (permeability of free space)
The oscillating J in the primary coil wire is the sole source of B
On the axis of a circular coil (magnetic dipole), the field at axial distance r is given by the Biot-Savart law integrated over the loop. For a single circular loop of radius a carrying current I, the on-axis field is:
On-Axis Field of a Single Circular Loop
B(r) = (μ₀Ia²) / (2(a² + r²)^(3/2))
a = loop radius · r = axial distance from loop center
For r ≫ a (far field): B(r) ≈ (μ₀/4π)(2m/r³)
m = Ia² = magnetic dipole moment. Field falls as 1/r³
The r³ denominator is the critical physical fact governing WPT range. Compare to the inverse-square law (1/r²) of light propagation — the magnetic near-field falls off with distance even faster, one extra power of r. Doubling the separation reduces the field to 1/8 of its original strength. This is why even a 2× increase in coil distance causes dramatic efficiency loss, as seen in both the particle visualization and the oscilloscope output.
For our multi-turn coil (N turns, radius a), the effective dipole moment is m = NIA, where A = πa² is the loop area. This means more turns and larger coil radius both directly increase the field strength and range — the fundamental design tradeoff in WPT: bigger coils with more turns transfer power further, but at the cost of size, weight, and cost.
🔵 In the simulation: The particle arc height and density both encode this r³ relationship. As Distance increases, arcs flatten and thin out. The arc geometry mirrors the actual equipotential lines of the magnetic dipole field around a real coil — they close in tight loops near the coil and spread wide in the mid-field zone.
05 / Efficiency & Distance
The Inverse-Cube
Drop-Off
The magnetic dipole field's r³ dependence directly governs the coupling coefficient k. As derived from Neumann's formula for mutual inductance between coaxial loops, the coupling coefficient for two identical circular coils of radius a separated by distance d is approximately:
Coupling Coefficient vs Separation (coaxial coils)
k(d) ≈ k₀ × (d₀/d)^2.8
d = center-to-center coil separation
d₀ = reference distance where k = k₀ (typically coil radius)
Exponent ~2.8–3.0 for coaxial circular coils of equal size
This simulation uses this exact model — check the COUPLING k readout
The steep r^2.8 falloff means WPT is practically limited to distances of roughly 1–3× the coil diameter for useful efficiency levels. Beyond this "mid-range," the kQ product drops below 1, and efficiency falls as (kQ)² — losing efficiency quadratically with further distance increase. Compare to light (inverse-square, 1/r²) or sound (1/r in certain geometries): the magnetic near-field is simply much more spatially confined.
Practical Efficiency vs Distance Model (this simulation)
η(d) = (kQ)² / (1 + (kQ)²)
where k = distCoupling × freqCoupling
distCoupling = 1 / (1 + (d/5.5)^2.8)
η_max ≈ 90% at d = 1 cm · η drops to ~50% at d ≈ 5.5 cm
η ≈ 5% at d = 15 cm · η ≈ 1% at d = 20 cm
Engineering countermeasures to the range problem include: Ferrite cores concentrate flux and can extend effective coupling by 2–4×. Relay coils (repeaters) placed mid-gap can extend range, acting like wireless power "routers." Beam-forming phased arrays of multiple coils can steer the field toward a receiver. Impedance matching networks keep the system at the optimal operating point as geometry changes. And adaptive frequency tuning can track the resonant frequency as mutual inductance shifts with position.
Interestingly, the steep distance falloff is also a valuable safety feature. Wireless chargers don't accidentally energize cardiac pacemakers across the room or interfere with other electronics beyond their immediate vicinity. Medical WPT systems for implantable devices (cochlear implants, retinal prosthetics, insulin pumps) deliberately use small, tightly-coupled coils held close to the skin — exploiting the short range for both efficiency and safety.
Standard Comparison — How fast does power drop?
WPT near-field: P ∝ 1/r⁶ (power ∝ k² ∝ 1/r^5.6)
Radio far-field: P ∝ 1/r² (inverse square law)
Sound (free field): P ∝ 1/r²
Gravity: F ∝ 1/r²
WPT coupling is dramatically more distance-sensitive than any of these
Halving distance → 64× stronger coupling power!
